Semantic Networks

Human Processing Model

The Human Processing Model explains how humans handle information, starting with a stimulus received through the senses and briefly stored in sensory memory. This information is perceived, processed in working memory, and compared with long-term memory to make decisions, after which the motor system produces an action and feedback completes the cycle. CNNs are inspired by this process, especially human visual perception, as they analyze images in layers—first detecting simple features like edges and then recognizing complex patterns and objects, similar to how the human brain processes visual information.

Fitt's Law

Fitts’ Law explains how long it takes for a user to point to and select a target, such as clicking a button or icon. It states that the time required to reach a target depends on two main factors: the distance to the target and the size of the target. Targets that are closer and larger are faster and easier to select, while smaller and farther targets take more time and effort. This law is widely used in Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) to design efficient interfaces, ensuring that frequently used buttons are large, well-spaced, and easy to reach, which improves speed, accuracy, and overall usability.

Top-Down Approach

This semantic model describes the sequence of cognitive processes starting with sensory input captured by sensory memory (visual and auditory), which feeds into perception. Perception interacts with short-term and long-term memory, where semantic memory helps assign meaning. Cognition advances through thinking, involving skill development and mental models. Reasoning—deductive, abductive, and inductive—is used to analyze information. When solving problems, we organize perceptions (gestalt), explore potential solutions (problem space), and use strategies like analogy. Overall, the process flows from sensory perception to memory, understanding, reasoning, and problem-solving, supporting learning and decision-making.

Bottom-Up Approach

The bottom-up approach of memory starts with sensory input, which is processed through perception and enters Short-Term Memory (STM). STM temporarily holds information and has a limited capacity described by Miller’s Law (7 ± 2 items). To manage this limit, information is organized using chunking, where smaller units are grouped into meaningful chunks. Because STM retains recent information more strongly, the recency effect occurs, allowing the last items to be recalled more easily. This approach highlights how memory builds upward from basic sensory input to higher cognitive processing.

Cognitive Processes

Cognitive processes include all processes underlying mental activities like attention, perception, learning, memory thinking, problem solving, reasoning.

  • Sensation – The process of detecting and receiving raw sensory stimuli from the environment.
  • Attention – The mental process of brining few stimuli into the center of awareness out of many stimuli present.
  • Perception – The interpretation of sensory input to make sense of the environment.
  • Learning – The acquisition of new knowledge, skills, or behaviors through experience.
  • Memory – The process of storing, retaining, and recalling information when needed.
  • Forgetting – The loss or inability to retrieve previously stored information.
  • Thinking – The mental process of organizing and analyzing information to form ideas or solutions.
  • Problem Solving – The process of identifying solutions to complex or unfamiliar situations.
  • Reasoning – The ability to draw conclusions and make inferences based on information.
  • Decision Making – The process of choosing the best option among several alternatives.

Automatic Syringe Design

The automatic syringe example is a good illustration of interface design in safety-critical systems. The old automatic syringe design was insufficient because it relied on a standard numeric input, forcing users to mentally calculate or map the dosage, which increased errors and cognitive load. After consulting users, the design was improved by adding increment/decrement controls for each digit, allowing doses to be adjusted directly and intuitively. This shows that good design matters; it reduces mistakes, aligns with human cognitive abilities, and ensures the device is safe, efficient, and easy to use, whereas poor design can lead to confusion and potential risks.
